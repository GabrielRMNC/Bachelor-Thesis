\renewcommand{\abstractname}{Abstract}
\begin{abstract}
The approximation of functions through polynomials is a foundational concept in numerical analysis, essential for evaluating complex functions using only the elementary arithmetic operations. This thesis explores the mathematical theory and the practical implementation of polynomial interpolation, focusing on the critical transition from equidistant to optimally distributed nodes. We begin by presenting the classical and the barycentric forms of Lagrange interpolation, followed by Newton's method using divided differences and finite (forward/backward) differences. Each method is accompanied by detailed worked examples carried out by hand.

The core of the paper investigates Runge's phenomenon, demonstrating how high-degree polynomial interpolation on equally spaced nodes leads to catastrophic divergence near the endpoints of the interval. To resolve this numerical instability, Chebyshev nodes are introduced and mathematically justified through their minimax (sup-norm minimizing) property. Finally, original MATLAB simulations are presented to validate the theoretical claims: using exclusively Newton's divided differences, we contrast the unbounded error obtained with equidistant nodes against the steady convergence obtained with Chebyshev nodes for the Runge function.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The ability to approximate complex mathematical functions using simpler, computable formulas is a cornerstone of numerical analysis and scientific computing. In practice, many functions encountered in engineering, physics, and computer science cannot be evaluated exactly; even elementary functions such as $\sqrt{x}$, $e^{x}$, $\log x$ and the trigonometric functions ultimately have to be reduced, on a computer, to the four arithmetic operations $+,\,-,\,\times,\,\div$. Combining these operations means that we can directly evaluate only polynomials and rational functions, so all other functions must be approximated by them \cite{micula4}. Interpolation provides a robust mechanism to construct a known function---typically a polynomial---that matches a discrete set of data points exactly, allowing for accurate approximations of the underlying unknown function.

The primary objective of this thesis is to explore the theoretical foundations of polynomial interpolation, examine its inherent numerical limitations, and demonstrate the methods that yield stable approximations. The paper is structured to guide the reader from classical definitions to practical computational implementations.

Chapter 2 introduces Lagrange interpolation, covering both its classical theoretical form and the computationally efficient barycentric formulation. Chapter 3 transitions to Newton's method, detailing divided differences and the forward and backward finite-difference formulas, which offer significant advantages when nodes are equidistant or when new data points are added. Chapter 4 highlights a fundamental limitation of polynomial interpolation, Runge's phenomenon, illustrating how increasing the degree of the interpolant on equidistant nodes paradoxically leads to massive divergence near the boundaries of the interval. Chapter 5 introduces the mathematical cure for this problem: Chebyshev nodes. By using the roots of Chebyshev polynomials, we show how the dominant factor in the interpolation error is minimized, restoring convergence. Finally, Chapter 6 presents practical applications: using custom MATLAB implementations, we perform numerical simulations that explicitly compare the error of equidistant nodes against that of Chebyshev nodes, confirming the theoretical claims of the preceding chapters.

\paragraph{Original contribution.} The personal contribution of the author consists of the unified and progressive structure of the work, the detailed step-by-step worked examples computed by hand for each interpolation method (linear and quadratic Lagrange, the barycentric formula, divided differences, and forward/backward differences), the independent MATLAB implementations of the barycentric Lagrange and Newton divided-difference algorithms, and the numerical experiments of Chapter 6 that quantitatively confirm the superiority of Chebyshev nodes over equidistant nodes for the Runge function.

\subsection{Historical Background and Applications}

The idea of interpolating tabulated values is ancient---it underlies the astronomical tables of antiquity---but its modern theory begins in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries with Isaac Newton, who introduced divided and finite differences, and with Joseph-Louis Lagrange, whose basis-polynomial formula gave the interpolant a closed form. A deeper question---whether a continuous function can be approximated arbitrarily well by polynomials---was answered affirmatively by Karl Weierstrass in 1885. However, Weierstrass's theorem only guarantees the \emph{existence} of a good approximating polynomial; it says nothing about whether the specific polynomial obtained by interpolating at a prescribed set of nodes will converge. That this convergence can fail was made dramatically clear by Carl Runge in 1901 and, in full generality, by the theorems of Faber and Bernstein in the following decade. The remedy, rooted in the approximation theory of Pafnuty Chebyshev, is the subject of this thesis.

Interpolation is far more than a curiosity. It is the foundation of numerical integration and differentiation (where an interpolant replaces the integrand or the function to be differentiated), of multistep methods for ordinary differential equations, and of computer-aided geometric design and computer graphics, where smooth curves and surfaces are reconstructed from a finite set of control points. In each of these settings the accuracy of the final result is inherited directly from the accuracy of the underlying interpolation, which makes the choice of nodes---the central question of this work---a matter of genuine practical importance.

\section{Lagrange Interpolation}

Approximation of functions is one of the most important tasks in numerical analysis. Most functions encountered in mathematical problems and applications cannot be evaluated exactly, even though we usually treat them as if they were completely known quantities \cite{micula4}. Interpolation provides a method to find a computable function---usually a polynomial---whose graph passes through a given set of data points.

\paragraph{Historical note.} Although the formula carries the name of Joseph-Louis Lagrange (1736--1813), who published it in 1795, equivalent constructions were known earlier to Edward Waring (1779) and Leonhard Euler. The barycentric reformulation discussed below is modern and was popularized in numerical practice by Berrut and Trefethen \cite{berrut}.

\subsection{Classical Lagrange Form}

\begin{definition}[Interpolation problem]
Given $n+1$ distinct points, called \emph{nodes}, $x_i \in [a,b]$ for $i = 0, \dots, n$, and the values $f(x_i) = y_i$ of an unknown function $f: [a,b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, we seek a polynomial $P(x)$ of minimum degree that satisfies the interpolation conditions
\begin{equation}\label{eq:interp-cond}
    P(x_i) = f(x_i), \qquad i = 0, \dots, n .
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

A first, naive approach is to look for the polynomial directly in the monomial basis, $P(x) = a_0 + a_1 x + \dots + a_n x^n$. Imposing the interpolation conditions \eqref{eq:interp-cond} gives a linear system for the unknown coefficients,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:vandermonde}
    \begin{pmatrix}
        1 & x_0 & \cdots & x_0^{\,n} \\
        1 & x_1 & \cdots & x_1^{\,n} \\
        \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\
        1 & x_n & \cdots & x_n^{\,n}
    \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix} a_0 \\ a_1 \\ \vdots \\ a_n \end{pmatrix}
    =
    \begin{pmatrix} y_0 \\ y_1 \\ \vdots \\ y_n \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
whose coefficient matrix is the \emph{Vandermonde matrix} \cite{heath}. Its determinant equals $\prod_{0 \le i < j \le n}(x_j - x_i)$, which is nonzero precisely because the nodes are distinct; the system therefore has a unique solution. This already establishes that an interpolating polynomial of degree at most $n$ exists and is unique. In practice, however, the Vandermonde system is notoriously ill-conditioned~\cite{barwolff}, and solving it costs $\mathcal{O}(n^3)$ operations. The Lagrange and Newton forms presented in this chapter avoid building this matrix altogether, which is why they are preferred. The same existence and uniqueness result is restated, in the form most convenient for what follows, below.

\begin{theorem}[Existence and Uniqueness]\label{thm:exist}
There is a unique polynomial $L_n f$ of degree at most $n$ satisfying the interpolation conditions \eqref{eq:interp-cond}. This polynomial can be written as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lagrange}
    L_n f(x) = \sum_{i=0}^n l_i(x)\, f(x_i),
\end{equation}
where $l_i(x)$ are the Lagrange fundamental (basis) polynomials, defined as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:basis}
    l_i(x) = \prod_{\substack{j=0 \\ j \neq i}}^n \frac{x - x_j}{x_i - x_j}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

The basis polynomials satisfy the property $l_i(x_j) = \delta_{ij}$ (equal to $1$ if $i=j$ and $0$ otherwise), which makes it immediate that \eqref{eq:lagrange} reproduces the data values $f(x_i)$.

\begin{example}[Fundamental basis polynomials, by hand]\label{ex:basis}
For the three nodes $x_0 = 0,\ x_1 = 1,\ x_2 = 2$, formula \eqref{eq:basis} gives
\begin{align*}
    l_0(x) &= \frac{(x-1)(x-2)}{(0-1)(0-2)} = \frac{(x-1)(x-2)}{2}, \\
    l_1(x) &= \frac{(x-0)(x-2)}{(1-0)(1-2)} = -x(x-2), \\
    l_2(x) &= \frac{(x-0)(x-1)}{(2-0)(2-1)} = \frac{x(x-1)}{2}.
\end{align*}
One checks directly that $l_0(0)=1$, $l_0(1)=l_0(2)=0$, and similarly for $l_1$ and $l_2$, confirming the property $l_i(x_j)=\delta_{ij}$. The interpolant is then the linear combination $L_2 f(x) = f_0\,l_0(x) + f_1\,l_1(x) + f_2\,l_2(x)$. Figure~\ref{fig:basis} plots the three basis polynomials; each equals $1$ at its own node and vanishes at the others.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Linear interpolation]
Consider two distinct nodes $(x_0, y_0)$ and $(x_1, y_1)$. The polynomial of degree $1$ that interpolates this data is
\begin{equation}
    P_1(x) = \frac{x - x_1}{x_0 - x_1}\, y_0 + \frac{x - x_0}{x_1 - x_0}\, y_1 .
\end{equation}
For instance, to interpolate $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$ at the nodes $x_0 = 1$ and $x_1 = 4$ (so $y_0=1,\ y_1=2$), substituting the values yields
\[
P_1(x) = \frac{x-4}{1-4}\cdot 1 + \frac{x-1}{4-1}\cdot 2
       = \frac{-(x-4) + 2(x-1)}{3} = \frac{1}{3}x + \frac{2}{3}.
\]
For three nodes the construction is analogous: adding the node $x_2 = 9$ (with $\sqrt{9}=3$) to the same data yields the quadratic interpolant $P_2(x) = -\tfrac{1}{60}x^2 + \tfrac{5}{12}x + \tfrac{3}{5}$, which is derived step by step in Example~\ref{ex:dd2}. Figure~\ref{fig:sqrt} compares the two interpolants with $f$ itself; each additional node visibly improves the approximation across the whole interval.
\end{example}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{fig_sqrt_interp.png}
    \caption{Linear ($P_1$, nodes $1,4$) and quadratic ($P_2$, nodes $1,4,9$) interpolation of $f(x)=\sqrt{x}$. Adding a node improves the fit over the whole interval.}
    \label{fig:sqrt}
\end{figure}

While the polynomial matches the function exactly at the interpolation nodes, an error exists at all other points within the interval. This error is described by the following classical result.

\begin{theorem}[Interpolation Error]\label{thm:error}
Let $f \in C^{n+1}[a,b]$ and consider the distinct nodes $x_0, \dots, x_n \in [a,b]$. Then for every $x \in [a,b]$ there exists a point $\xi \in (a,b)$ such that the interpolation remainder is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:remainder}
    (R_n f)(x) = f(x) - (L_n f)(x) = \frac{u(x)}{(n+1)!}\, f^{(n+1)}(\xi),
\end{equation}
where $u(x) = (x - x_0)(x - x_1) \cdots (x - x_n)$ is the nodal polynomial.
\end{theorem}

A useful consequence of \eqref{eq:remainder} is the uniform bound
\begin{equation}\label{eq:errorbound}
    |(R_n f)(x)| \le \frac{|u(x)|}{(n+1)!}\, M_{n+1}, \qquad
    M_{n+1} = \sup_{t\in[a,b]} \bigl|f^{(n+1)}(t)\bigr| .
\end{equation}
The two factors on the right-hand side are essentially independent: the first depends only on the placement of the nodes through $u(x)$, while the second depends only on the function $f$. This separation will be the key to understanding both Runge's phenomenon (Chapter 4) and its cure through Chebyshev nodes (Chapter 5).

\subsection{Approximation-Theoretic Background}

It is natural to expect that taking more and more interpolation nodes will drive the error to zero. The following two classical results show that the situation is more subtle, and they motivate the central problem of this thesis.

\begin{theorem}[Weierstrass Approximation Theorem]\label{thm:weierstrass}
Let $f:[a,b]\to\mathbb{R}$ be continuous. Then for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists a polynomial $p$ such that $\displaystyle\max_{x\in[a,b]} |f(x) - p(x)| < \varepsilon$.
\end{theorem}

Weierstrass's theorem guarantees that \emph{some} polynomial approximates $f$ as closely as desired, but it does not say how to find it, and in particular it does \emph{not} say that the interpolant at a given set of nodes will do so. The next result, due to Faber and Bernstein, shows that no single choice of nodes works for every continuous function \cite{schatzman}.

\begin{theorem}[Faber]\label{thm:faber}
For every prescribed triangular array of interpolation nodes in $[a,b]$, there exists a continuous function $f \in C[a,b]$ whose sequence of interpolating polynomials does not converge uniformly to $f$ on $[a,b]$ \cite{micula4}.
\end{theorem}

A concrete illustration is Bernstein's example $f(x) = |x|$ on $[-1,1]$: with equidistant nodes the interpolation error tends to infinity at every point except $x = -1, 0, 1$ \cite{micula4}. These results explain why the choice of nodes is decisive and foreshadow Runge's phenomenon, studied in Chapter 4.

\subsection{Barycentric Interpolation}

The classical Lagrange formula is well suited for theoretical purposes, but it is less desirable for practical computation \cite{micula4}. Evaluating it requires $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ floating-point operations for every value of $x$, adding a new node requires restarting the calculation from scratch, and the procedure is numerically unstable.

To improve efficiency, we introduce the \emph{barycentric weights}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:weights}
    w_i = \frac{1}{\displaystyle\prod_{\substack{j=0 \\ j \neq i}}^n (x_i - x_j)}, \qquad i = 0, 1, \dots, n .
\end{equation}
Using these weights, the interpolant can first be written in the \textbf{first barycentric formula}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bary1}
    L_n f(x) = u(x) \sum_{i=0}^n \frac{w_i}{x - x_i}\, f_i .
\end{equation}
Once the weights are known, evaluating $L_n f(x)$ requires only $\mathcal{O}(n)$ operations, and adding a new node updates the weights cheaply. Applying \eqref{eq:bary1} to the constant function $f \equiv 1$, whose interpolant is $1$ by uniqueness, gives $1 = u(x)\sum_i w_i/(x-x_i)$. Dividing \eqref{eq:bary1} by this identity eliminates $u(x)$ and yields the \textbf{second barycentric formula}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bary2}
    L_n f(x) = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=0}^n \frac{w_i}{x - x_i}\, f_i}{\displaystyle\sum_{i=0}^n \frac{w_i}{x - x_i}} .
\end{equation}
This form is particularly elegant: the weights appear identically in the numerator and denominator (except for the factors $f_i$), so any common factor in all the weights $w_i$ may be canceled without changing the value of $L_n f$ \cite{micula4, berrut}. This property is exploited in Chapter 5.

\begin{example}[First barycentric formula, by hand]\label{ex:bary1}
Using the same data $f(x)=x^2$ at $x_0=0,\,x_1=1,\,x_2=2$ and the weights $w_0=\tfrac12,\,w_1=-1,\,w_2=\tfrac12$ (computed in detail in Example~\ref{ex:bary}), the first formula \eqref{eq:bary1} evaluates as follows at $x=1.5$. The nodal polynomial is $u(1.5)=(1.5)(0.5)(-0.5)=-0.375$, and the sum is $\sum_i \tfrac{w_i}{1.5-x_i}f_i = \tfrac13(0) + (-2)(1) + (-1)(4) = -6$, so
\[
    L_2(1.5) = u(1.5)\sum_{i}\frac{w_i}{1.5-x_i}f_i = (-0.375)(-6) = 2.25 = f(1.5).
\]
The second formula below removes the explicit factor $u(x)$ by dividing by the analogous sum without the $f_i$.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Second barycentric formula, by hand]\label{ex:bary}
Let us interpolate $f(x) = x^2$ using the three nodes $x_0 = 0,\ x_1 = 1,\ x_2 = 2$, with values $f_0 = 0,\ f_1 = 1,\ f_2 = 4$, and evaluate the result at $x = 1.5$.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step 1 --- barycentric weights} from \eqref{eq:weights}:
\begin{align*}
    w_0 &= \frac{1}{(0 - 1)(0 - 2)} = \frac{1}{2}, \\
    w_1 &= \frac{1}{(1 - 0)(1 - 2)} = -1, \\
    w_2 &= \frac{1}{(2 - 0)(2 - 1)} = \frac{1}{2}.
\end{align*}

\noindent\textit{Step 2 --- the terms} $\dfrac{w_i}{x - x_i}$ at $x = 1.5$:
\begin{align*}
    i=0&: \quad \frac{1/2}{\,1.5 - 0\,} = \frac{1}{3}, &
    i=1&: \quad \frac{-1}{\,1.5 - 1\,} = -2, &
    i=2&: \quad \frac{1/2}{\,1.5 - 2\,} = -1 .
\end{align*}

\noindent\textit{Step 3 --- the second barycentric formula} \eqref{eq:bary2}:
\begin{equation*}
    L_2(1.5) = \frac{\frac{1}{3}(0) + (-2)(1) + (-1)(4)}{\frac{1}{3} + (-2) + (-1)}
             = \frac{-6}{-8/3} = \frac{18}{8} = 2.25 .
\end{equation*}
This matches the exact value $f(1.5) = 1.5^2 = 2.25$ exactly, as it must, since the unique quadratic interpolant of three points sampled from a quadratic is the quadratic itself.
\end{example}

\subsection{Implementation Notes}

When implementing the second barycentric formula \eqref{eq:bary2} programmatically, one critical edge case must be handled. The expression is formally undefined when $x$ exactly equals one of the nodes $x_i$, because the term $w_i/(x-x_i)$ produces a division by zero in both the numerator and the denominator \cite{micula4}. Mathematically, an application of L'H\^opital's rule shows that the limit as $x \to x_i$ equals exactly $f(x_i)$, so a continuous extension to the nodes is justified. A robust implementation must therefore check whether $x$ coincides with a node (within machine-precision tolerance) and, if so, return the corresponding value $f(x_i)$ directly. The MATLAB function presented in Chapter 6 implements precisely this safeguard.

\section{Newton Interpolation}

While the Lagrange form elegantly proves the existence and uniqueness of the interpolation polynomial, it has a significant computational drawback: if a new data point is added, the entire polynomial must be recomputed from scratch \cite{micula5, runge}. Newton's interpolation method resolves this by expressing the polynomial in a hierarchical form, in which adding a new node simply appends a new term without altering the previously computed coefficients.

\paragraph{Historical note.} The method originates with Isaac Newton (1643--1727), who developed both the divided-difference formula for unequally spaced data and the forward/backward finite-difference formulas for tabulated values with a constant step. These finite-difference tables were a central computational tool for centuries before the advent of electronic computers.

\subsection{Divided Differences}

Newton's interpolation polynomial relies on the concept of \emph{divided differences}. Given $n+1$ pairs $(x_k, y_k)$ for $k = 0, \dots, n$, the divided differences are defined recursively as follows \cite{isaacson, micula5}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Zeroth order:} $f[x_k] = f(x_k) = y_k$;
    \item \textbf{First order:} $\displaystyle f[x_k, x_{k+1}] = \frac{f[x_{k+1}] - f[x_k]}{x_{k+1} - x_k}$;
    \item \textbf{$i$-th order:} $\displaystyle f[x_k, \dots, x_{k+i}] = \frac{f[x_{k+1}, \dots, x_{k+i}] - f[x_k, \dots, x_{k+i-1}]}{x_{k+i} - x_k}$.
\end{itemize}

Using these coefficients, Newton's interpolation polynomial $N_n f(x)$ of degree at most $n$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:newton}
    N_n f(x) = f[x_0] + \sum_{i=1}^n f[x_0, \dots, x_i] \prod_{j=0}^{i-1} (x - x_j).
\end{equation}
By the uniqueness of the interpolation polynomial (Theorem~\ref{thm:exist}), $N_n f(x)$ is identical to the Lagrange polynomial $L_n f(x)$ for the same set of nodes; the two are merely written in different algebraic forms \cite{micula5}.

\begin{theorem}[Interpolation error, Newton's form]\label{thm:newton-error}
The remainder of the Newton interpolation polynomial can be written, using a divided difference that includes the evaluation point $x$, as
\begin{equation}
    R_n f(x) = f[x, x_0, \dots, x_n]\,(x - x_0)(x - x_1) \cdots (x - x_n),
\end{equation}
which, for $f \in C^{n+1}$, coincides with the remainder \eqref{eq:remainder} of Theorem~\ref{thm:error}, where $\xi$ lies in the smallest interval containing the nodes $x_0, \dots, x_n$ and the point $x$ \cite{micula5}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}
Divided differences are tied to derivatives by the mean-value relation $f[x_0,\dots,x_n] = \dfrac{f^{(n)}(\zeta)}{n!}$ for some $\zeta$ in the smallest interval containing the nodes. In particular, divided differences of order higher than the degree of a polynomial vanish; this is why the third-order difference of $x^3$ in Example~\ref{ex:dd1} is the constant $1$ (its leading coefficient) and a fourth-order difference would be $0$.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Divided differences, by hand --- equidistant nodes]\label{ex:dd1}
Let us interpolate $f(x) = x^3$ at the nodes $x_0 = 0,\ x_1 = 1,\ x_2 = 2,\ x_3 = 3$, with function values $0, 1, 8, 27$. We construct the divided-difference table; each entry is the difference of the two entries immediately to its left, divided by the difference of the corresponding outermost nodes.

\begin{center}

\begin{tabular}{c|c|c|c|c}
$x_k$ & $f[x_k]$ & 1st order & 2nd order & 3rd order \\
\hline
$0$ & $0$  &                       &                         & \\
    &      & $\frac{1-0}{1-0}=1$   &                         & \\
$1$ & $1$  &                       & $\frac{7-1}{2-0}=3$     & \\
    &      & $\frac{8-1}{2-1}=7$   &                         & $\frac{6-3}{3-0}=1$ \\
$2$ & $8$  &                       & $\frac{19-7}{3-1}=6$    & \\
    &      & $\frac{27-8}{3-2}=19$ &                         & \\
$3$ & $27$ &                       &                         & \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The Newton coefficients are the topmost entry in each column: $D_0 = 0,\ D_1 = 1,\ D_2 = 3,\ D_3 = 1$. Hence
\begin{equation*}
    N_3(x) = 0 + 1\,(x) + 3\,(x)(x-1) + 1\,(x)(x-1)(x-2),
\end{equation*}
which expands exactly to $x^3$. Evaluating at $x = 1.5$,
\[
N_3(1.5) = 1.5 + 3(1.5)(0.5) + (1.5)(0.5)(-0.5) = 1.5 + 2.25 - 0.375 = 3.375 = 1.5^3 .
\]
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Divided differences, by hand --- non-equidistant nodes]\label{ex:dd2}
A major advantage of divided differences over the finite-difference formulas below is that the nodes need not be equally spaced. We interpolate $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$ at the nodes $x_0 = 1,\ x_1 = 4,\ x_2 = 9$, with values $1, 2, 3$:
\begin{align*}
    f[x_0, x_1] &= \frac{2-1}{4-1} = \frac{1}{3}, &
    f[x_1, x_2] &= \frac{3-2}{9-4} = \frac{1}{5}, \\[2pt]
    f[x_0, x_1, x_2] &= \frac{\tfrac{1}{5}-\tfrac{1}{3}}{9-1} = \frac{-2/15}{8} = -\frac{1}{60}. &&
\end{align*}
The Newton polynomial is
\[
    N_2(x) = 1 + \tfrac{1}{3}(x-1) - \tfrac{1}{60}(x-1)(x-4) = -\tfrac{1}{60}x^2 + \tfrac{5}{12}x + \tfrac{3}{5}.
\]
As a check, $N_2(9) = -\tfrac{81}{60} + \tfrac{45}{12} + \tfrac{3}{5} = 3 = \sqrt{9}$, and at the off-node point $x=2$ we obtain $N_2(2) = \tfrac{41}{30} \approx 1.3667$, a reasonable approximation of $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.4142$.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Adding a node without recomputation]\label{ex:addnode}
The chief practical advantage of Newton's form is that a new node merely \emph{appends} one term. Continuing the previous example for $f(x)=\sqrt{x}$, suppose we add the node $x_3 = 16$ (with $\sqrt{16}=4$). The coefficients already computed, $f[x_0],\,f[x_0,x_1],\,f[x_0,x_1,x_2]$, remain valid; only the new leading coefficient must be found. The additional divided differences are
\[
    f[x_2,x_3] = \frac{4-3}{16-9} = \frac{1}{7}, \qquad
    f[x_1,x_2,x_3] = \frac{\tfrac{1}{7}-\tfrac{1}{5}}{16-4} = -\frac{1}{210},
\]
\[
    f[x_0,x_1,x_2,x_3] = \frac{-\tfrac{1}{210}-\left(-\tfrac{1}{60}\right)}{16-1} = \frac{1}{1260}.
\]
Hence the cubic interpolant is obtained simply by adding one term to the quadratic $N_2$ of Example~\ref{ex:dd2}:
\[
    N_3(x) = N_2(x) + \tfrac{1}{1260}(x-1)(x-4)(x-9).
\]
As a check, at $x = 16$ the new term contributes $\tfrac{1}{1260}(15)(12)(7) = 1$, so $N_3(16) = N_2(16) + 1 = 3 + 1 = 4 = \sqrt{16}$. With the Lagrange form, by contrast, all basis polynomials would have to be rebuilt from scratch.
\end{example}

\subsection{Forward and Backward Differences}

When the interpolation nodes are equally spaced, with $x_i = x_0 + i h$ for a constant step size $h$, computing general divided differences is unnecessary; the polynomial can be built from simpler \emph{finite differences} \cite{micula5}.

\textbf{Newton's forward-difference formula} uses the forward-difference operator $\Delta^k f_i = \Delta^{k-1} f_{i+1} - \Delta^{k-1} f_i$, with $\Delta^0 f_i = f_i$. Substituting $s = (x - x_0)/h$, the interpolant becomes
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fwd}
    L_n f(x) = f_0 + \binom{s}{1}\Delta f_0 + \binom{s}{2}\Delta^2 f_0 + \dots + \binom{s}{n}\Delta^n f_0,
\end{equation}
where $\binom{s}{k} = \tfrac{s(s-1)\cdots(s-k+1)}{k!}$ is the generalized binomial coefficient. This formula attains its highest precision near the beginning of the table (close to $x_0$) \cite{micula5}.

\textbf{Newton's backward-difference formula} uses the backward-difference operator $\nabla^k f_i = \nabla^{k-1} f_i - \nabla^{k-1} f_{i-1}$. With the substitution $t = (x - x_n)/h$, it reads
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bwd}
    L_n f(x) = f_n + \frac{t}{1!}\nabla f_n + \frac{t(t+1)}{2!}\nabla^2 f_n + \dots + \frac{t(t+1)\cdots(t+n-1)}{n!}\nabla^n f_n .
\end{equation}
Conversely, the backward formula is preferred when evaluating points near the end of the table (close to $x_n$) \cite{micula5}.

\begin{example}[Forward differences, by hand]\label{ex:fwd}
Using again $f(x) = x^3$ on the equidistant nodes $x_0 = 0,\ x_1 = 1,\ x_2 = 2,\ x_3 = 3$ (step $h = 1$), the forward differences are
\begin{align*}
    \Delta^0 f &= [\,0,\ 1,\ 8,\ 27\,], &
    \Delta^1 f &= [\,1,\ 7,\ 19\,], \\
    \Delta^2 f &= [\,6,\ 12\,], &
    \Delta^3 f &= [\,6\,].
\end{align*}
To evaluate at $x = 1.5$ we set $s = (1.5 - 0)/1 = 1.5$ and use the leading entries $\Delta^k f_0$:
\begin{equation*}
    P(1.5) = 0 + 1.5(1) + \frac{1.5(1.5-1)}{2}(6) + \frac{1.5(1.5-1)(1.5-2)}{6}(6) = 1.5 + 2.25 - 0.375 = 3.375,
\end{equation*}
in agreement with Example~\ref{ex:dd1} and with $1.5^3 = 3.375$.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Backward differences, by hand]\label{ex:bwd}
For the same data, the relevant backward differences anchored at the last node are $f_3 = 27,\ \nabla f_3 = 19,\ \nabla^2 f_3 = 12,\ \nabla^3 f_3 = 6$. To evaluate near the end of the table, at $x = 2.5$, we set $t = (2.5 - 3)/1 = -0.5$ and apply \eqref{eq:bwd}:
\begin{align*}
    P(2.5) &= 27 + \frac{-0.5}{1}(19) + \frac{(-0.5)(0.5)}{2}(12) + \frac{(-0.5)(0.5)(1.5)}{6}(6) \\
           &= 27 - 9.5 - 1.5 - 0.375 = 15.625 = 2.5^3 .
\end{align*}
\end{example}

\subsection{Implementation Notes}

From a software perspective, generating Newton's polynomial is a two-step process. First, the divided differences are computed and stored; this is typically done with a one-dimensional array updated in place to save memory, requiring $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ operations, after which the array holds the coefficients $D_i = f[x_0, \dots, x_i]$ \cite{micula5}. Second, the polynomial is evaluated at a given point $x$. To reduce the cost of each evaluation to $\mathcal{O}(n)$ and to limit floating-point rounding, the polynomial is evaluated using a nested (Horner-like) scheme:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:horner}
    N_n f(x) = D_0 + (x - x_0)\Bigl[D_1 + (x - x_1)\bigl[D_2 + \dots + (x - x_{n-1})D_n\bigr]\Bigr].
\end{equation}
This nested form is exactly the one implemented in the MATLAB routine of Chapter 6.

\newpage
\section{Runge's Phenomenon}

In polynomial interpolation it is natural to assume that increasing the number of nodes---and hence the degree of the interpolating polynomial---will yield a more accurate approximation of the underlying function. However, this intuitive assumption is false for certain functions, particularly when using equidistant nodes. This counterintuitive behavior is known as \emph{Runge's phenomenon} \cite{runge}.

\paragraph{Historical note.} The effect was described by the German mathematician Carl David Tolm\'e Runge (1856--1927) in 1901, while he was studying the polynomial interpolation of a simple rational function on equally spaced nodes.

\subsection{The Runge Function}

Runge investigated the interpolation of the function
\begin{equation}\label{eq:runge}
    f(x) = \frac{1}{1 + 25x^2}
\end{equation}
on the interval $[-1, 1]$. This expression is now commonly referred to as the \emph{Runge function} \cite{runge}.

\subsection{Behavior with Equidistant Nodes}

When the Runge function is interpolated by a polynomial $P_n(x)$ of degree $n$ on $n+1$ equidistant nodes, the polynomial matches the function exactly at the data points. However, between the nodes---and especially near the boundaries $x = \pm 1$---the polynomial develops severe oscillations \cite{runge, knorrenschild}. As the number of nodes grows, instead of converging to $f$, the magnitude of these oscillations increases without bound:
\begin{equation}
    \lim_{n \to \infty} \left( \max_{-1 \le x \le 1} |f(x) - P_n(x)| \right) = \infty .
\end{equation}
Figure~\ref{fig:progression} illustrates this divergence for $6$, $11$, and $16$ equidistant nodes: the central region is approximated reasonably well, but the oscillations near the endpoints become dramatically worse as the degree increases.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig_runge_progression.png}
    \caption{Interpolation of the Runge function on equidistant nodes for increasing degree. The error near the endpoints grows rapidly with the number of nodes.}
    \label{fig:progression}
\end{figure}

\subsection{The Cause of the Phenomenon}

The root cause lies in the structure of the error term. Recall from Theorem~\ref{thm:error} and the bound \eqref{eq:errorbound} that the error is controlled by the product of two factors: the nodal polynomial $u(x) = \prod_{i=0}^n (x - x_i)$, which depends only on the nodes, and the high-order derivative $f^{(n+1)}$, which depends only on the function. For equidistant nodes the amplitude of $u(x)$ becomes enormous near the edges of the interval as $n$ grows---its peaks near the endpoints exceed its values near the center by a factor that grows roughly like $2^n$. At the same time, the higher-order derivatives of the Runge function also grow rapidly \cite{runge}. The combination of these two effects causes the error to explode near the boundaries, overwhelming the good approximation achieved in the middle of the interval.

This analysis points directly to the remedy: since the troublesome factor $u(x)$ depends only on the placement of the nodes, one should abandon equidistant nodes in favor of a distribution that keeps $\max_{[-1,1]} |u(x)|$ as small as possible. That optimal distribution is the subject of the next chapter.

\newpage
\section{Chebyshev Nodes}

As Runge's phenomenon demonstrates, polynomial interpolation on equally spaced nodes can diverge catastrophically near the boundaries. To guarantee convergence and minimize the maximum interpolation error, the nodes should be clustered more densely near the endpoints than in the middle. The most mathematically sound and widely used distribution for this purpose is the set of \emph{Chebyshev nodes} \cite{runge, knorrenschild}.

\paragraph{Historical note.} Chebyshev polynomials are named after the Russian mathematician Pafnuty Lvovich Chebyshev (1821--1894), a founder of approximation theory, whose study of least-deviation polynomials leads directly to the optimality property below.

\subsection{Chebyshev Polynomials of the First Kind}

The foundation of these nodes lies in the Chebyshev polynomials of the first kind, denoted $T_n(x)$. For $x \in [-1, 1]$ they are defined by the trigonometric relation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cheb}
    T_n(x) = \cos\!\left(n \arccos x\right), \qquad n = 0, 1, 2, \dots
\end{equation}
Despite their trigonometric appearance, these are genuine polynomials: $T_0(x) = 1$, $T_1(x) = x$, and they satisfy the three-term recurrence
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cheb-rec}
    T_{n+1}(x) = 2x\,T_n(x) - T_{n-1}(x),
\end{equation}
so that $T_2(x) = 2x^2 - 1$, $T_3(x) = 4x^3 - 3x$, and so on. The leading coefficient of $T_n$ is $2^{n-1}$.

These polynomials possess a remarkable \emph{minimax} property: among all \emph{monic} polynomials of degree $n$ (polynomials with leading coefficient $1$), the normalized Chebyshev polynomial $\widetilde T_n(x) = 2^{1-n} T_n(x)$ has the smallest possible maximum absolute value on $[-1,1]$, namely $2^{1-n}$. In other words, no monic degree-$n$ polynomial stays closer to zero across the whole interval. This is a direct consequence of the equioscillation visible in Figure~\ref{fig:chebpolys}: on $[-1,1]$ each $T_n$ attains the extreme values $\pm 1$ exactly $n+1$ times.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig_chebyshev_polys.png}
    \caption{The first six Chebyshev polynomials of the first kind. On $[-1,1]$ each $T_n$ oscillates between $-1$ and $+1$, attaining these extreme values $n+1$ times (the equioscillation property).}
    \label{fig:chebpolys}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Chebyshev Nodes of the First Kind}

The Chebyshev nodes are defined as the roots of $T_{n+1}(x)$. For an interpolating polynomial of degree $n$ there are $n+1$ such roots on the standard interval $[-1, 1]$, given by \cite{runge}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cheb-nodes}
    x_k = \cos\!\left(\frac{2k+1}{2n+2}\,\pi\right), \qquad k = 0, 1, \dots, n .
\end{equation}
Geometrically, these are the projections onto the $x$-axis of $n+1$ equally spaced points on the upper half of the unit circle; this is exactly why they cluster near the endpoints, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:nodes}. For a function defined on an arbitrary interval $[a, b]$, the nodes are mapped from $[-1,1]$ by the affine transformation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cheb-map}
    x_k^{[a,b]} = \frac{a+b}{2} + \frac{b-a}{2}\cos\!\left(\frac{2k+1}{2n+2}\,\pi\right).
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{fig_nodes_distribution.png}
    \caption{Distribution of $15$ equidistant nodes (squares) versus $15$ Chebyshev nodes (circles), obtained by projecting equally spaced points on the unit circle. The Chebyshev nodes cluster near the endpoints.}
    \label{fig:nodes}
\end{figure}

\begin{example}[Chebyshev nodes, by hand]\label{ex:cheb}
We compute the Chebyshev nodes for a quadratic interpolant ($n = 2$, hence $3$ nodes) on $[-1,1]$. From \eqref{eq:cheb-nodes} with $2n+2 = 6$,
\[
    x_k = \cos\!\left(\frac{(2k+1)\pi}{6}\right), \quad k = 0,1,2
    \ \Longrightarrow\
    x_0 = \cos\tfrac{\pi}{6} = \tfrac{\sqrt3}{2},\quad
    x_1 = \cos\tfrac{\pi}{2} = 0,\quad
    x_2 = \cos\tfrac{5\pi}{6} = -\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2}.
\]
The corresponding nodal polynomial is $u(x) = \bigl(x-\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2}\bigr)\,x\,\bigl(x+\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2}\bigr) = x^3 - \tfrac{3}{4}x$, which is exactly the normalized Chebyshev polynomial $\widetilde T_3(x) = \tfrac{1}{4}T_3(x) = \tfrac{1}{4}(4x^3 - 3x)$. Its maximum modulus on $[-1,1]$ is $\tfrac{1}{4} = \tfrac{1}{2^{n}}$ with $n = 2$, in agreement with Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax}.

If instead we interpolate on the interval $[a,b] = [0,4]$, the affine map \eqref{eq:cheb-map} gives $x_k = 2 + 2\cos\!\bigl(\tfrac{(2k+1)\pi}{6}\bigr)$, namely $\{\,3.732,\ 2,\ 0.268\,\}$, again clustered toward the endpoints of $[0,4]$.
\end{example}

\subsection{Minimizing the Interpolation Error}

The advantage of Chebyshev nodes follows directly from the error term. Recall from Theorem~\ref{thm:error} that the error depends on the nodal polynomial $u(x) = \prod_{i=0}^n (x - x_i)$, which is \emph{monic} of degree $n+1$. By the minimax property, the choice of nodes that minimizes $\max_{[-1,1]}|u(x)|$ is precisely the one that makes $u$ equal to the normalized Chebyshev polynomial $\widetilde T_{n+1}$.

\begin{theorem}[Minimax property of Chebyshev nodes]\label{thm:minimax}
If $x_0, \dots, x_n$ are the roots of the Chebyshev polynomial $T_{n+1}(x)$, then the nodal polynomial satisfies
\begin{equation}
    \max_{-1 \le x \le 1} \left| \prod_{i=0}^n (x - x_i) \right| = \frac{1}{2^{n}},
\end{equation}
and this is the smallest possible value of $\max_{[-1,1]}|u(x)|$ over all choices of $n+1$ nodes in $[-1,1]$.
\end{theorem}

By replacing equidistant nodes with Chebyshev nodes, the amplitude of $u(x)$ is thus reduced to its minimum, which removes the explosive oscillations near the boundaries and acts as the standard cure for Runge's phenomenon \cite{knorrenschild}. On the general interval $[a,b]$ the corresponding bound becomes
\[
    \|R_n f\|_\infty \le \frac{(b-a)^{\,n+1}}{2^{\,2n+1}(n+1)!}\,\|f^{(n+1)}\|_\infty .
\]
Figure~\ref{fig:nodal} makes this concrete: for $N=11$ nodes the equidistant nodal polynomial reaches large peaks near $x=\pm1$, whereas the Chebyshev nodal polynomial equioscillates with the uniformly small amplitude $1/2^{n}$ guaranteed by Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{fig_nodal_polynomial.png}
    \caption{Magnitude of the nodal polynomial $|u(x)|$ for $11$ equidistant nodes versus $11$ Chebyshev nodes. The equidistant peaks near the endpoints are roughly nine times larger than the uniform Chebyshev amplitude $1/2^{n}$ (dotted line); this is precisely the mechanism behind Runge's phenomenon and its cure.}
    \label{fig:nodal}
\end{figure}

\begin{remark}[Lebesgue constant]
The sensitivity of interpolation to a node set is measured by its \emph{Lebesgue constant} $\Lambda_n = \max_{x\in[-1,1]} \sum_{i=0}^n |l_i(x)|$, which bounds how much the interpolation error can exceed the best possible polynomial approximation. For equidistant nodes $\Lambda_n$ grows exponentially (roughly like $2^{n}/(n\log n)$), reflecting the instability seen above, whereas for Chebyshev nodes it grows only logarithmically (roughly like $\tfrac{2}{\pi}\log n$). This contrast is a second, quantitative way of expressing the near-optimality of Chebyshev nodes.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Chebyshev Nodes of the Second Kind}

A closely related family is provided by the Chebyshev polynomials of the second kind, $U_n(x) = \dfrac{\sin\bigl((n+1)\arccos x\bigr)}{\sin(\arccos x)}$, which obey the same recurrence \eqref{eq:cheb-rec} but start from $U_0(x) = 1,\ U_1(x) = 2x$. Their roots,
\begin{equation}
    x_k = \cos\!\left(\frac{k\pi}{n+1}\right), \qquad k = 1, \dots, n,
\end{equation}
together with the two endpoints $\pm 1$, give the so-called Chebyshev points of the second kind. These are widely used in practice---they are the extrema of $T_n$ and lead to especially simple barycentric weights, $w_j = (-1)^j$ for interior points and $w_j = \tfrac{1}{2}(-1)^j$ at the two endpoints \cite{micula4}. Both families cluster near the endpoints and cure Runge's phenomenon; in this thesis we use the first-kind nodes \eqref{eq:cheb-nodes} for the simulations.

\subsection{Barycentric Interpolation with Chebyshev Nodes}

Chebyshev nodes pair exceptionally well with the second barycentric formula \eqref{eq:bary2}. For equidistant nodes the barycentric weights $w_j$ vary by exponentially large factors (of order roughly $2^n$), which amplifies rounding errors and causes instability \cite{micula4}. For Chebyshev points of the first kind, by contrast, after canceling common factors independent of $j$ the weights simplify to \cite{micula4}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cheb-weights}
    w_j = (-1)^j \sin\!\left(\frac{(2j+1)\pi}{2n+2}\right), \qquad j = 0, \dots, n .
\end{equation}
Because these weights vary only by factors of $\mathcal{O}(n)$ rather than exponentially, the barycentric evaluation remains well conditioned, so small data perturbations near the center do not translate into large oscillations near the edges \cite{micula4, berrut}.

\newpage
\section{Applications and Numerical Simulations}

To demonstrate the concepts of the previous chapters, we implement the interpolation algorithms in MATLAB. The aim is to simulate Runge's phenomenon numerically and to observe how replacing equidistant nodes with Chebyshev nodes minimizes the interpolation error. In line with the numerical objectives of this thesis, the error comparison is carried out using \emph{only} Newton's divided-difference method.

\subsection{MATLAB Implementations}

We first implement the second barycentric formula for Lagrange interpolation. The routine computes the weights in $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ operations and then evaluates the polynomial; the exact-node singularity ($x = x_i$) is handled explicitly to avoid division by zero, as discussed in Chapter 2.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Barycentric Lagrange interpolation.}, label={lst:bary}]
function y_eval = barycentric_interp(x_nodes, y_nodes, x_eval)
    n = length(x_nodes);
    w = ones(1, n);
    % Compute barycentric weights w_i = 1 / prod_{j~=i}(x_i - x_j)
    for i = 1:n
        for j = 1:n
            if i ~= j
                w(i) = w(i) / (x_nodes(i) - x_nodes(j));
            end
        end
    end

    y_eval = zeros(size(x_eval));
    % Evaluate the second barycentric formula
    for k = 1:length(x_eval)
        exact_match = find(x_nodes == x_eval(k), 1);
        if ~isempty(exact_match)
            y_eval(k) = y_nodes(exact_match);   % continuous extension at a node
        else
            num = sum((w .* y_nodes) ./ (x_eval(k) - x_nodes));
            den = sum( w             ./ (x_eval(k) - x_nodes));
            y_eval(k) = num / den;
        end
    end
end
\end{lstlisting}

Next we implement Newton's method using divided differences. The routine builds the divided-difference coefficients in place (storing only the diagonal to save memory) and evaluates the polynomial through the nested Horner scheme \eqref{eq:horner}. This is the routine used for all error comparisons below.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Newton interpolation via divided differences.}, label={lst:newton}]
function y_eval = newton_div_diff(x_nodes, y_nodes, x_eval)
    n = length(x_nodes);
    D = y_nodes;                 % initialize with 0th-order differences

    % Compute divided differences in place
    for i = 2:n
        for j = n:-1:i
            D(j) = (D(j) - D(j-1)) / (x_nodes(j) - x_nodes(j-i+1));
        end
    end

    y_eval = zeros(size(x_eval));
    % Evaluate using Horner's nested scheme
    for k = 1:length(x_eval)
        val = D(n);
        for i = n-1:-1:1
            val = D(i) + (x_eval(k) - x_nodes(i)) * val;
        end
        y_eval(k) = val;
    end
end
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Error Comparison: Equidistant vs. Chebyshev Nodes}

To expose Runge's phenomenon, we interpolate the Runge function \eqref{eq:runge} on $[-1,1]$ using \texttt{newton\_div\_diff}, and compare the maximum absolute error obtained with $N$ equidistant nodes against that obtained with $N$ Chebyshev nodes given by \eqref{eq:cheb-nodes}.

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Simulation of Runge's phenomenon and error comparison.}, label={lst:sim}]
f = @(x) 1 ./ (1 + 25 * x.^2);
x_eval  = linspace(-1, 1, 2000);   % dense grid for measuring the error
y_exact = f(x_eval);

N_values = [5, 7, 9, 11, 13, 15, 21, 31];

fprintf('  N |  Equidistant error |  Chebyshev error\n');
for N = N_values
    % 1. Equidistant nodes
    x_eq = linspace(-1, 1, N);
    p_eq = newton_div_diff(x_eq, f(x_eq), x_eval);
    err_eq = max(abs(y_exact - p_eq));

    % 2. Chebyshev nodes of the first kind
    k = 0:N-1;
    x_cheb = cos((2*k + 1) * pi / (2*N));
    p_cheb = newton_div_diff(x_cheb, f(x_cheb), x_eval);
    err_cheb = max(abs(y_exact - p_cheb));

    fprintf('%3d | %18.4e | %16.4e\n', N, err_eq, err_cheb);
end
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{A Second Test Function: the Bernstein Example}

To show that the advantage of Chebyshev nodes is not specific to the Runge function, we repeat the experiment for the Bernstein example $f(x) = |x|$ on $[-1,1]$. The only change to the simulation code is the definition of the target function, \texttt{f = @(x) abs(x);}\ ; the \texttt{newton\_div\_diff} routine is used exactly as before. The pattern is the same as for the Runge function: the equidistant error ultimately explodes, reaching order $10^{4}$ at $N=31$, whereas the Chebyshev error decreases. This is consistent with the error bound \eqref{eq:errorbound}: because $|x|$ is not differentiable at the origin, the factor $M_{n+1} = \sup |f^{(n+1)}|$ is not controlled, so even the optimal placement of nodes cannot deliver fast convergence. Chebyshev nodes remove the catastrophic instability caused by the nodal polynomial $u(x)$, but they cannot compensate for a lack of smoothness in $f$ itself.

\subsection{Verification and Computational Cost}

The correctness of the two implementations was checked in several independent ways. First, on any common set of nodes the \texttt{barycentric\_interp} and \texttt{newton\_div\_diff} routines return the same values (up to rounding), as they must by the uniqueness of the interpolation polynomial (Theorem~\ref{thm:exist}). Second, the routines reproduce the worked examples computed by hand in the earlier chapters: \texttt{barycentric\_interp} returns $2.25$ for the data of Example~\ref{ex:bary}, and \texttt{newton\_div\_diff} returns $3.375$ for the data of Example~\ref{ex:dd1}, matching the analytical results exactly. Third, every error value in Table~\ref{tab:errors} was confirmed to be monotone in the expected direction---increasing for equidistant nodes, decreasing for Chebyshev nodes.

Regarding efficiency, both algorithms split naturally into a setup phase and an evaluation phase. Computing the barycentric weights, or the divided-difference coefficients, costs $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ operations and is performed once per node set. Each subsequent evaluation then costs only $\mathcal{O}(n)$: the second barycentric formula \eqref{eq:bary2} sums $n+1$ terms, and Newton's polynomial is evaluated with $n$ multiplications and $n$ additions through the nested Horner scheme \eqref{eq:horner}. This separation is what makes both forms far more practical than the classical Lagrange formula \eqref{eq:lagrange}, which costs $\mathcal{O}(n^2)$ for \emph{every} evaluation point.

\section{Conclusions}

This thesis examined polynomial interpolation from both a theoretical and a computational perspective. Starting from the classical Lagrange form and its efficient barycentric reformulation, we then presented Newton's divided differences and the forward/backward finite-difference formulas, illustrating every method with worked examples computed by hand. We saw that all of these constructions produce, for a given set of nodes, the same unique interpolating polynomial; they differ only in algebraic form and in computational convenience.

The central theme of the work was the contrast between two choices of nodes. Runge's phenomenon shows that increasing the degree of the interpolant on equidistant nodes can make the approximation arbitrarily worse, because the nodal polynomial $u(x)$ in the error term grows exponentially near the endpoints. Chebyshev nodes resolve this by minimizing $\max|u(x)|$ through their minimax property, restoring convergence. The MATLAB experiments of Chapter 6, carried out exclusively with Newton's divided differences, confirmed this quantitatively: for the Runge function the equidistant error grew beyond $10^3$ while the Chebyshev error fell to the order of $10^{-3}$ over the same range of node counts.

A natural continuation of this work would be to study other remedies for the instability of high-degree interpolation, such as piecewise polynomial (spline) interpolation, and to extend the comparison to other test functions and to the conditioning of the barycentric weights.

From a practical standpoint, the results suggest a clear recommendation. When the modeller is free to choose where the function is sampled, Chebyshev nodes (combined with the stable barycentric evaluation) should be preferred over equidistant nodes, especially for moderate to high degrees. When the data are given on a fixed equidistant grid---as often happens with measurements---raising the polynomial degree indefinitely is counterproductive; in that setting low-degree piecewise (spline) interpolation is usually the safer choice. In all cases, the error formula \eqref{eq:errorbound} provides the unifying explanation: the achievable accuracy is governed by the size of the nodal polynomial $u(x)$, and controlling that quantity is the essence of good interpolation.
